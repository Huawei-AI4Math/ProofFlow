Informal theorem and proof
Theorem: If $\{F_n\}$ converges uniformly to $F$ on $S$ and each $F_n$ is continuous at a point $x_0$ in $S,$ then so is $F$. Similar statements hold for continuity from the right and left$.$ Proof: Suppose that each $F_n$ is continuous at $x_0$. If $x\in S$ and $n\ge1$, then \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.4.8} \begin{array}{rcl} |F(x)-F(x_0)|\ar\le |F(x)-F_n(x)|+|F_n(x)-F_n(x_0)|+|F_n(x_0)-F(x_0)| \\ \ar\le |F_n(x)-F_n(x_0)|+2\|F_n-F\|_S. \end{array} \end{equation} Suppose that $\epsilon>0$. Since $\{F_n\}$ converges uniformly to $F$ on $S$, we can choose $n$ so that $\|F_n-F\|_S<\epsilon$. For this fixed $n$, \eqref{eq:4.4.8} implies that \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.4.9} |F(x)-F(x_0)|<|F_n(x)-F_n(x_0)|+2\epsilon,\quad x\in S. \end{equation} Since $F_n$ is continuous at $x_0$, there is a $\delta>0$ such that $$ |F_n(x)-F_n(x_0)|<\epsilon\mbox{\quad if\quad} |x-x_0|<\delta, $$ so, from \eqref{eq:4.4.9}, $$ |F(x)-F(x_0)|<3\epsilon,\mbox{\quad if\quad} |x-x_0|<\delta. $$ Therefore, $F$ is continuous at $x_0$. Similar arguments apply to the assertions on continuity from the right and left.
